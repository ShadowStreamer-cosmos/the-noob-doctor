01. Pathophysiology

// The "why" behind everything
🦠 Etiology — What Causes MS?

Rheumatic fever → most common cause worldwide (>99% in developing countries like India). RF causes leaflet thickening, commissural fusion, subvalvular fusion, and calcification over years-decades.


Other rare causes: Congenital MS (Lutembacher syndrome = congenital MS + ASD), carcinoid syndrome (right-sided valves mostly), SLE (Libman-Sacks), mucopolysaccharidoses.

⚠ EXAM TRAP

Rheumatic fever affects the MITRAL valve most commonly, followed by Aortic > Tricuspid > Pulmonary. Remember: MAP-T (Mitral, Aortic, Pulmonary, Tricuspid — in order of frequency).


RF causes stenosis in the mitral valve but can cause regurgitation + stenosis in others. The mitral valve is preferentially stenosed because it bears the greatest diastolic pressure load.

⚡ Hemodynamic Cascade — The Domino Effect
↓ Mitral Valve Area (Normal: 4–6 cm²)
LA cannot empty → ↑ Left Atrial Pressure
↑ Pulmonary Venous Pressure → Pulmonary Congestion
Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH)
RV Pressure Overload → RV Failure (Cor Pulmonale)
↑ RA → Tricuspid Regurgitation (functional)
⭐ KEY HEMODYNAMIC FACT
  • Normal MVA = 4–6 cm². Symptoms begin when MVA < 2 cm²
  • Severe MS = MVA < 1 cm². Critical = MVA < 0.6 cm²
  • LA pressure must rise 10–20 mmHg above LV diastolic pressure to push blood through
  • CO remains normal at rest until very late (compensated by ↑ HR) — but ↑ HR worsens MS (less diastolic filling time)!

⚠ PRESSURE HALF-TIME — Exam Favourite
MVA (cm²) = 220 / Pressure Half-Time (ms)
PHT normal = ~60 ms | PHT in severe MS = >220 ms
Longer PHT = Smaller MVA = More Severe

Think: pressure takes longer to equalize because there's a tighter obstruction → PHT ↑ as severity ↑

🧠 Why Does MS Get Worse With Tachycardia?

In MS, blood flow from LA to LV depends on the duration of diastole. During tachycardia (e.g., AF, exercise, fever, pregnancy):


↑ Heart Rate → ↓ Diastolic filling time → LA cannot empty → ↑↑ LAP → acute pulmonary edema


This is why atrial fibrillation (most common complication of MS) can precipitate acute decompensation — loss of atrial kick + fast ventricular rate = double blow.

🔍 Physical Examination — Sign by Sign

General & Peripheral

Malar flush (mitral facies) — Bilateral dusky-pink flushing of cheeks. Due to low CO → peripheral vasodilation → dilated capillaries. Seen in severe MS with low output state.


Pulse

Low volume pulse. Irregularly irregular in AF. Pulse deficit present in AF (apical rate > radial rate).


JVP

Elevated with prominent a-wave (if sinus rhythm, due to RV stiffness from PAH). In AF → a-wave absent. Giant v-waves suggest functional TR.

⚠ APEX BEAT TRAP — INICET Favourite

Apex beat in MS is NOT displaced — the LV is small/normal (because it's underfilled). The apex feels "tapping" due to palpable loud S1. If the apex is displaced outward and downward → suggests associated MR or AS (LV enlargement).

🏥 CLINICAL CASE 1
A 28-year-old woman presents with progressive exertional dyspnea and palpitations for 3 months. She had recurrent throat infections as a child treated with penicillin. On exam: irregularly irregular pulse, malar flush, tapping apex, parasternal heave. Auscultation reveals a loud S1, opening snap, and a low-pitched diastolic rumble.
Q: What is the most likely diagnosis, and what does the presence of parasternal heave indicate?
✓ ANSWER & REASONING

Diagnosis: Rheumatic Mitral Stenosis with Atrial Fibrillation and Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension

Parasternal heave = RV hypertrophy/enlargement → indicates significant PAH has developed. This is a bad prognostic sign.

Tapping apex = palpable loud S1. Malar flush = low CO state. AF = irregularly irregular pulse in a young woman with rheumatic history = classic MS presentation.

2D Echo + Doppler is the investigation of choice for diagnosing and grading MS severity.

⚠ PHT vs Planimetry — When Each Is Used

Planimetry = most accurate, used when image quality good. Underestimates MVA after BMV.
PHT = affected by LV compliance, AR, acute settings. Unreliable immediately after BMV (compliance changes).
Continuity equation & PISA = alternative methods.

🧮 Wilkins Score — BMV Decision Maker

Used to decide if patient is suitable for Balloon Mitral Valvuloplasty (BMV). Each parameter scored 1–4:

Leaflet Mobility
1 = highly mobile, pliable → 4 = minimal movement
Leaflet Thickening
1 = near-normal (4–5mm) → 4 = severe (>8–10mm)
Subvalvular Thickening
1 = minimal → 4 = severe with extensive chordae involvement
Calcification
1 = single area → 4 = extensive throughout leaflets
Score ≤8 → Suitable for BMV ✓ Score >8 → Surgery preferred ✗
⭐ TEE BEFORE BMV — Always Remember
  • Transesophageal Echo (TEE) is mandatory before BMV to rule out LA thrombus (especially LA appendage)
  • LA thrombus = absolute contraindication to BMV
  • If thrombus found → 3 months anticoagulation → repeat TEE → BMV if thrombus resolved
🏥 CLINICAL CASE 2 — INICET Style
A 32-year-old woman with rheumatic MS is being evaluated for balloon mitral valvuloplasty. Echocardiography shows: MVA by PHT = 0.9 cm², leaflet mobility score 2, leaflet thickening 2, subvalvular thickening 2, calcification 1. TEE shows no LA thrombus.
Q: What is the Wilkins score? Is she suitable for BMV?
✓ ANSWER

Wilkins score = 2+2+2+1 = 7 → Score ≤ 8 → SUITABLE for BMV

MVA of 0.9 cm² = severe MS, and score is favorable. No LA thrombus on TEE. She can proceed with BMV.

Bonus: Even though MVA < 1 cm² is severe, BMV is the treatment of choice here because of favorable anatomy and young age.

💥 Loud S1 — Why?

In MS, the mitral leaflets remain wide open in diastole (due to ↑ LAP pushing them open). When ventricular systole starts, the leaflets snap shut abruptly from a wide-open position → loud, "tapping" S1.

⚠ WHEN S1 BECOMES SOFT IN MS

S1 becomes soft/inaudible when:

1. Severe calcification of mitral leaflets (immobile leaflets can't snap loudly)
2. Long PR interval (leaflets drift back to near-closed position before systole)
3. Severe MR coexisting
4. This means: SOFT S1 in MS = more severe/calcified disease

🎯 Opening Snap — The Most Tested Sound in MS
What is it?
High-pitched early diastolic sound after S2; snapping open of stiffened (but mobile) MV leaflets
Where heard best?
Medial to apex; also well heard at left sternal border with diaphragm
S2-OS interval
SHORTER = MORE SEVERE (inversely proportional to severity)
Why shorter = severe?
Higher LAP → aortic valve closes → pressure equalization is faster → valve snaps open sooner after S2
When is OS absent?
Severe calcification (immobile leaflets cannot snap), congenital MS, Wilkins score > 8
Mimics
S3 (lower pitch, later, at apex), pericardial knock (after pericardiectomy, constriction), P2 (at left 2nd ICS)
⭐ KEY: S2-OS INTERVAL FACTS
  • Normal/mild MS: S2-OS interval = 80–100 ms (longer, LAP not very high)
  • Severe MS: S2-OS interval = 30–60 ms (shorter, LAP very high)
  • Mnemonic: "SEVERE = SHORTER snap"
  • On exertion: interval becomes shorter (LAP rises with exertion)
🌊 Mid-Diastolic Rumble — The Murmur of MS
Character
Low-pitched, rumbling, diastolic murmur
Timing
Mid-diastolic; begins after OS
Location
Apex only (localized; does NOT radiate)
Position
Left lateral decubitus position enhances it (apex closer to chest wall)
Instrument
Bell of stethoscope (low-frequency sounds)
Presystolic accentuation
Increase at end of diastole due to atrial contraction → ABSENT in AF
Duration matters
Longer murmur = more severe (more time in diastole with gradient)
Maneuver to enhance
Exercise (↑ CO, ↑ HR briefly); Amyl nitrite (↑ HR); Left lateral decubitus
⚠ PRESYSTOLIC ACCENTUATION — TRAP!

Presystolic accentuation (PSA) = crescendo increase in murmur intensity at end of diastole just before S1. It is due to atrial contraction squeezing blood through narrow mitral valve.


In AF: PSA is ABSENT because there's no coordinated atrial contraction.


However, some older studies suggest PSA can occur in AF (due to increased flow velocity as LV contracts). For exams: PSA = ABSENT in AF.

05. Management

// Medical, Interventional, Surgical — Know the Decision Tree
💊 Medical Management
Drug/Intervention Indication Mechanism / Notes
Beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol) Rate control in AF or sinus tachycardia ↓ HR → ↑ diastolic filling time → ↓ LAP; drug of choice for rate control in MS
Digoxin Rate control in AF (especially with HF) ↑ AV nodal refractory period; also mild positive inotropy
Diuretics (furosemide) Pulmonary congestion symptoms ↓ preload → ↓ LAP → ↓ dyspnea; avoid over-diuresis (↓ CO)
Warfarin anticoagulation AF + MS; LA thrombus; prior embolism Target INR 2–3; NOT NOACs (NOACs not approved for AF in mechanical valves or severe MS)
RF prophylaxis All rheumatic MS Monthly Benzathine Penicillin IM; prevents further RF attacks; continue until age 40 or 10 years after last RF episode
⚠ TRAP: ACE INHIBITORS / ARBs IN MS

ACEi/ARBs are generally NOT beneficial in isolated MS (no LV dysfunction to treat). They cause vasodilation → ↑ reflex HR → worsens MS. However, they may be used for concomitant HTN.

🎈 Balloon Mitral Valvuloplasty (BMV / PTMC)

Percutaneous Transvenous Mitral Commissurotomy (PTMC) — the procedure of choice for suitable MS patients.


Indications

  • ▸ Symptomatic MS (NYHA II–IV) with MVA ≤ 1.5 cm² AND Wilkins score ≤ 8
  • ▸ Asymptomatic MS with MVA < 1 cm²
  • ▸ MS with new-onset AF
  • ▸ Severe MS in pregnancy (preferred over surgery)

Contraindications

Absolute Contraindication Relative Contraindication
LA thrombus (especially appendage) Mild-moderate MR coexisting
Moderate-severe MR Suboptimal anatomy (high Wilkins score)
Wilkins score > 8 Prior BMV with restenosis
Severe calcification Severe PAH (relative)
⭐ BMV PROCEDURE FACTS
  • Transseptal puncture → balloon inflated across MV → commissures split
  • Inoue balloon technique = most commonly used
  • Endpoint: MVA > 1.5 cm² OR mean gradient < 5 mmHg
  • Most common complication: MR (due to leaflet tear)
  • Success rate: ~80–95% in favorable anatomy
🔪 Surgical Management
Procedure Indication Notes
Closed Mitral Valvotomy (CMV) Pliable valve without calcification; limited settings Blind procedure using Tubbs dilator via left thoracotomy; historical; still done in India in some centers
Open Mitral Commissurotomy (OMC) Calcified/thickened valve; better results than CMV On cardiopulmonary bypass; direct visualization; better than CMV for moderate calcification
Mitral Valve Replacement (MVR) Unsuitable for BMV/commissurotomy; severe MR; Wilkins >8; failed BMV Mechanical (lifelong anticoagulation) vs Bioprosthetic (no anticoagulation after 3 months; wears out in 10–15 years)
🤰 MS in Pregnancy — HIGH YIELD!

Pregnancy causes ↑ blood volume (30–50%), ↑ HR, ↑ CO → all worsen MS significantly.


Critical period: 28–32 weeks of gestation (maximum hemodynamic stress) + labor/delivery + early postpartum period.

Management Details
Rate control Beta-blockers (metoprolol); avoid atenolol (IUGR risk)
Anticoagulation LMWH in 1st trimester and near term; Warfarin in 2nd trimester (safer period)
BMV in pregnancy Preferred if MVA < 1.5 cm² and symptomatic; done after 20 weeks; lead apron shielding
Surgery Last resort; high fetal mortality (~25%)
Delivery Vaginal preferred; epidural analgesia; avoid tachycardia; avoid Valsalva
⚠ TRAP: MS vs MR in Pregnancy

MS WORSENS in pregnancy (↑ HR + ↑ volume = ↑ LAP across stenosis)
MR TOLERATES pregnancy better (↓ SVR in pregnancy ↓ regurgitant fraction)

06. MCQ Drilldown

// 10 high-yield questions — INICET / USMLE / NEET PG calibre
Q 01
A 30-year-old woman with rheumatic mitral stenosis develops acute-onset dyspnea and palpitations. Her pulse is 130/min, irregularly irregular. Which of the following explains the acute deterioration?
  • A Decreased systemic vascular resistance
  • B Loss of atrial kick + reduced diastolic filling time
  • C Increased left ventricular end-diastolic volume
  • D Decreased left atrial pressure
✓ ANSWER: B

New onset AF causes two simultaneous problems: (1) Loss of atrial kick (25–30% of CO) and (2) Tachycardia reduces diastolic filling time → LA cannot empty → ↑↑ LAP → acute pulmonary edema.

A — ↓SVR would actually help MS (less afterload). B is correct.

C — LV is underfilled in MS (not overfilled). LV is small/normal.

D — LAP actually INCREASES (not decreases) causing pulmonary edema.

Q 02
In a patient with mitral stenosis, the S2-opening snap interval is 40 ms. What does this indicate?
  • A Mild MS with low left atrial pressure
  • B Pliable, mobile mitral valve leaflets
  • C Severe MS with high left atrial pressure
  • D The opening snap is a normal finding
✓ ANSWER: C

S2-OS interval is INVERSELY proportional to LA pressure and severity of MS. A short interval (40 ms) = high LAP = severe MS. After S2, the higher the LAP, the faster it equilibrates with the LV pressure that has just fallen → mitral valve snaps open sooner → shorter interval.

A — Low LAP = longer S2-OS interval (mild MS).

B — Pliable leaflets allow an OS to be present, but mobility doesn't determine the interval length.

D — Opening snap is always pathological.

Q 03
A 35-year-old woman with known MS presents with hoarseness of voice. Laryngoscopy shows left vocal cord palsy. What is the most likely cause?
  • A Thyroid enlargement compressing the nerve
  • B Left recurrent laryngeal nerve compression by enlarged left atrium
  • C Right recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy
  • D Aortic aneurysm
✓ ANSWER: B — Ortner's Syndrome

Ortner's (cardiovocal) syndrome = hoarseness due to enlarged left atrium compressing the left recurrent laryngeal nerve as it loops under the aortic arch. Classic complication of severe MS with massive LA enlargement.

A — Thyroid would not cause this in the context of MS.

C — The RIGHT recurrent laryngeal nerve loops around the right subclavian artery, not near the LA.

D — Aortic aneurysm can cause Ortner's but is far less common than MS-related LA enlargement.

Q 04
A patient with MS and AF is found to have a left atrial thrombus on TEE. They are currently on warfarin with INR 1.5 (subtherapeutic). What is the most appropriate next step?
  • A Proceed immediately with BMV
  • B Switch to NOAC
  • C Optimize warfarin (INR 2–3), recheck TEE after 3 months, then proceed with BMV
  • D Perform emergency MVR
✓ ANSWER: C

LA thrombus is an absolute contraindication to BMV (risk of embolization during procedure). Correct approach: therapeutic anticoagulation (warfarin, target INR 2–3) for at least 3 months → repeat TEE → if thrombus has resolved → proceed with BMV.

A — Catastrophic risk of systemic embolism. Never proceed with LA thrombus present.

B — NOACs (rivaroxaban, apixaban) are NOT approved for AF with severe rheumatic MS (INVICTUS trial showed worse outcomes). Use warfarin.

D — Surgery is not an emergency here and is not the first step.

Q 05
A 28-year-old woman at 30 weeks gestation with rheumatic MS (MVA 1.0 cm²) develops NYHA class III dyspnea despite maximal medical therapy. What is the best intervention?
  • A Emergency Caesarean section
  • B Mitral valve replacement on cardiopulmonary bypass
  • C Percutaneous balloon mitral valvuloplasty (BMV)
  • D Closed mitral valvotomy
✓ ANSWER: C

BMV (PTMC) is the preferred intervention in pregnant women with severe MS who fail medical therapy. It is done after 20 weeks, with lead apron shielding to protect the fetus. It avoids cardiopulmonary bypass (which has ~25% fetal mortality).

A — C-section only addresses delivery, not the underlying valve problem. At 30 weeks, the fetus is premature.

B — MVR requires CPB → very high fetal mortality (~25%). Reserved only as last resort.

D — Closed mitral valvotomy is largely historical and inferior to BMV.

Q 06
A 45-year-old man with MS is found to have a Wilkins score of 10. He has no LA thrombus, no significant MR, but is symptomatic (NYHA III). What is the most appropriate treatment?
  • A BMV (Balloon Mitral Valvuloplasty)
  • B Mitral valve replacement surgery
  • C Closed mitral valvotomy
  • D Continue medical therapy and reassess in 6 months
✓ ANSWER: B

Wilkins score >8 = unfavorable anatomy for BMV (heavy calcification, restricted mobility, subvalvular disease). The preferred treatment here is surgical MVR. Open mitral commissurotomy may be possible if there is minimal calcification, but MVR is usually the definitive answer when Wilkins >8.

A — BMV in Wilkins >8 has poor outcomes and high risk of MR. Contraindicated.

C — Closed valvotomy = historical, inferior results even for suitable anatomy.

D — Patient is symptomatic NYHA III and has severe MS. Medical therapy alone is insufficient.

Q 07
A patient with rheumatic MS has a mid-diastolic rumble at the apex. On examination, presystolic accentuation (PSA) is absent. The most likely reason is:
  • A Mild MS
  • B Increased left atrial pressure
  • C Atrial fibrillation
  • D Pliable mitral leaflets
✓ ANSWER: C

PSA (presystolic accentuation) is caused by atrial contraction squeezing blood through the narrowed mitral valve at end-diastole. In AF, there is no coordinated atrial contraction → PSA disappears. This is a classic and frequently tested exam point.

A — Mild MS would have PSA intact (atria still functioning in sinus rhythm).

B — ↑ LAP increases the transvalvular gradient but doesn't remove PSA; it actually makes the murmur louder overall.

D — Leaflet pliability affects opening snap, not presystolic accentuation.

Q 08
A 40-year-old woman with severe MS (MVA 0.8 cm²) in sinus rhythm is asymptomatic. What is the most appropriate management?
  • A Annual follow-up only
  • B Start warfarin anticoagulation immediately
  • C BMV if anatomy favorable, even though asymptomatic
  • D MVR immediately
✓ ANSWER: C

According to guidelines (AHA/ACC): Intervention is recommended for asymptomatic severe MS (MVA <1 cm²) even without symptoms, because very tight MS carries risk of sudden deterioration, AF, and embolism. If anatomy favorable (Wilkins ≤8, no thrombus) → BMV is preferred.

A — Watchful waiting is appropriate for mild/moderate MS, not severe (MVA <1 cm²).

B — Anticoagulation is indicated for AF with MS or prior embolism, not just severe MS in sinus rhythm without thrombus.

D — Surgery (MVR) is reserved when BMV is not feasible.

Q 09
A patient with long-standing MS has Graham Steell murmur. Which of the following best describes this murmur?
  • A Mid-diastolic rumble due to aortic regurgitation jet hitting the mitral valve
  • B Mid-diastolic murmur of acute rheumatic fever
  • C Early diastolic murmur of pulmonary regurgitation secondary to pulmonary hypertension
  • D Pansystolic murmur due to mitral regurgitation
✓ ANSWER: C

Graham Steell murmur = early diastolic, decrescendo murmur heard at the left sternal border (2nd–4th ICS), due to pulmonary regurgitation secondary to PAH. The pulmonary valve is structurally normal; it regurgitates because the pulmonary artery is dilated from high pressure.

A — This describes Austin Flint murmur (AR jet → MV flutter → mid-diastolic rumble).

B — This describes Carey Coombs murmur (acute rheumatic fever).

D — Pansystolic murmur of MR is a separate finding.

Q 10
On CXR of a patient with MS, you note elevation of the left main bronchus, a double density at the right cardiac border, and prominent upper lobe vessels. What additional finding would suggest severe pulmonary hypertension?
  • A Kerley B lines
  • B Pulmonary venous congestion
  • C Dilated main pulmonary artery and right heart enlargement
  • D Straightening of left heart border
✓ ANSWER: C

The question asks specifically about PAH features on CXR. PAH → dilated main pulmonary artery (prominent PA knuckle) + RV enlargement (boot-shaped heart or filling of retrosternal space). Pruning of peripheral pulmonary vessels is also seen.

A — Kerley B lines = interstitial pulmonary edema from ↑ pulmonary venous pressure, not specifically PAH.

B — Pulmonary venous congestion (upper lobe diversion) indicates elevated LAP — earlier finding before PAH.

D — Straightening of left border = LA appendage enlargement, present in any degree of MS.

07. Special Scenarios

// Edge cases and clinical scenarios that trip up even good students
⚡ MS + AF — The Dangerous Duo

AF complicates MS in 30–40% of patients. It is the most common complication and most common cause of hospitalization.


Scenario Management
New-onset AF with hemodynamic compromise DC cardioversion + anticoagulate + treat MS
Chronic AF with MS (rate control adequate) Rate control (beta-blocker ± digoxin) + Warfarin (INR 2–3); NOACs contraindicated in moderate-severe rheumatic MS
AF after successful BMV Sinus rhythm may restore; anticoagulate for at least 3–6 months post-BMV; if AF >1 year or LA very large, unlikely to restore SR
⚠ NOAC vs WARFARIN IN MS — INICET/USMLE TRAP

The INVICTUS trial (2022) specifically tested rivaroxaban vs warfarin in AF with rheumatic heart disease (mostly MS). Result: Rivaroxaban was INFERIOR to warfarin — higher stroke + systemic embolism + death. Therefore: Warfarin remains standard of care for AF in MS. NOACs are NOT approved here.

🏥 Lutembacher Syndrome

Congenital ASD + Rheumatic MS = Lutembacher syndrome. The ASD is usually of secundum type. The combination creates a left-to-right shunt that decompresses the LA (so symptoms of MS are modified — less dyspnea than expected), but right heart dilation is exaggerated.

⭐ EXAM POINTS
  • LA pressure is lower than expected (ASD allows LA decompression)
  • Loud S1 and opening snap may be softer than expected
  • Right heart is grossly enlarged (↑ shunt flow)
  • Treatment: Close ASD surgically + address MS (BMV or MVR)
💊 Rheumatic Fever Prophylaxis — The Timeline
Group Duration
RF without carditis 5 years or until age 21 (whichever longer)
RF with carditis, no residual heart disease 10 years or until age 21
RF with carditis + residual heart disease (valvular) 10 years or until age 40 (whichever longer); some say lifelong

Drug of choice: Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 MU IM every 3–4 weeks. Alternatively: Penicillin V 250 mg PO BD or Erythromycin 250 mg PO BD (if allergic).

🏥 CLINICAL CASE 3 — USMLE Step 2 Style
A 55-year-old man with known MS presents with progressive dyspnea. He has irregularly irregular pulse at 100/min, loud P2, parasternal heave, and a low-pitched diastolic murmur at the apex. Echo shows MVA 1.2 cm², Wilkins score 12, moderate MR, no LA thrombus. Pulmonary artery systolic pressure = 65 mmHg.
Q: What is the most appropriate next step in management?
✓ ANSWER & ANALYSIS

MVR (Mitral Valve Replacement) — BMV is contraindicated here because:

1. Wilkins score = 12 (>8 = unfavorable anatomy)
2. Moderate MR is a relative-to-absolute contraindication for BMV (risk of tearing → severe MR)
3. High Wilkins score indicates heavy calcification/subvalvular disease — poor BMV result expected

PAH (PA pressure 65 mmHg) will improve after valve replacement in many patients if it is reactive (not fixed fibrous PAH).

📊 MS vs MR vs AS — Quick Differentiator
Mitral Stenosis
  • Murmur: Mid-diastolic rumble
  • S1: Loud (→ soft if calcified)
  • Extra sound: Opening snap
  • Apex: Tapping, not displaced
  • LV size: Normal/small
  • Main complication: AF, embolism
Mitral Regurgitation
  • Murmur: Pansystolic, to axilla
  • S1: Soft/absent
  • Extra sound: S3
  • Apex: Displaced, hyperdynamic
  • LV size: Enlarged
  • Main complication: LV failure
Aortic Stenosis
  • Murmur: Ejection systolic, to neck
  • S1: Normal
  • Extra sound: Reverse split S2
  • Apex: Heaving, not displaced
  • LV size: Concentric hypertrophy
  • Main complication: Syncope, angina, HF
