Section 01

01. The Big Picture — What Even Is This?

Aortic Stenosis (AS) is the narrowing of the aortic valve, obstructing outflow from the Left Ventricle (LV) to the aorta. It's the most common valvular heart disease in developed countries and one of the highest-yield topics in cardiology exams.

🧠 The Genius Analogy Imagine the LV is a bouncer trying to push a crowd through a doorway. Aortic Stenosis is like that doorway getting progressively narrower. The bouncer (LV) has to push harder and harder — and eventually gets exhausted. That's concentric hypertrophy → diastolic dysfunction → failure.

The bouncer (LV) works harder as the door (aortic valve) gets narrower

Section 02

02. Pathophysiology — The Domino Effect

This is where it all connects. Understand the cascade, and the clinical features make perfect sense.

Step 1
Valve narrows → Increased resistance to LV outflow → Pressure overload on LV (not volume overload!)
Step 2
Concentric LV hypertrophy — wall thickens to compensate (Laplace's law: ↑wall thickness reduces wall stress)
Step 3
Diastolic dysfunction — thick, stiff ventricle can't relax properly → Elevated LVEDP → Pulmonary congestion
Step 4
Fixed cardiac output — narrowed valve limits forward flow → symptoms on exertion, then at rest
Step 5 (Late)
LV dilation + systolic failure — the "exhausted bouncer" → EF drops → Cardiogenic shock territory
💡 KEY DISTINCTION AS = Pressure overload → Concentric hypertrophy (wall ↑, cavity same/small)
AR/MR = Volume overload → Eccentric hypertrophy (cavity dilates)

On echo: In AS you'll see ↑wall thickness. In AR you'll see ↑LV cavity size.
⚡ Why Angina in AS? (Even without CAD!) 3 reasons:
1. Hypertrophied muscle = more O₂ demand
2. Compressed subendocardial vessels (↑LVEDP compresses from inside)
3. ↓Perfusion pressure across coronary ostia (narrow valve limits diastolic runoff)
In fact ~50% of AS patients with angina have normal coronaries.
Section 03

03. Clinical Features — The Holy Trinity + Signs

The Most Important Mnemonic in AS
S · A · D
  • SSyncope — mean survival 3 years after onset
  • AAngina — mean survival 5 years after onset
  • DDyspnea (Heart Failure) — mean survival 1–2 years after onset
💡 SAD = Sad prognosis. Dyspnea = worst prognosis (shortest survival). This is extremely high-yield for MCQs asking "which symptom indicates worst prognosis?"
🩺 Pulse

Slow-rising, plateau (anacrotic) pulse — also called "Pulsus parvus et tardus" (small and slow). The narrow valve slows and dampens the pressure wave.

🎯 High Yield
💓 Apex Beat

Heaving, sustained, undisplaced apex — heaving because of hypertrophy; undisplaced because no dilation (concentric, not eccentric).

🎯 High Yield
🫀 BP

Narrow pulse pressure — because forward flow is limited (low SBP) + compensatory peripheral resistance maintained (DBP normal/elevated).

🖐️ Thrill

Systolic thrill palpable in the aortic area (2nd RICS) and/or carotids. A "purring cat" sensation.

🔑 Signs of Severity — What Examiners Love
  • Soft/absent A2 (calcified valve)
  • Long, late-peaking murmur
  • S4 gallop (stiff LV)
  • Narrow pulse pressure
  • Reverse splitting of S2
  • Slow-rising pulse
  • LV heave
  • Exertional syncope
⚠️ CRITICAL EXAM TRAP: AS vs HOCM
Both cause exertional syncope + harsh systolic murmur + LVH. How to differentiate?

AS murmur: Increases with Squatting (↑venous return → ↑gradient), decreases with standing
HOCM murmur: Increases with Valsalva + Standing (↓venous return → worse obstruction)

Also: AS has slow-rising pulse, HOCM has bifid (spike-and-dome) pulse.

Section 05

05. Investigations — What You'll Be Asked

🫀 ECG Findings
  • LVH (Sokolow-Lyon criteria)
  • ST depression (strain pattern)
  • Left axis deviation
  • LBBB (advanced)
  • T-wave inversion (leads I, aVL, V5-V6)
  • P-mitrale (LAE)
📻 CXR Findings
  • Post-stenotic aortic dilation
  • Normal heart size (early)
  • Cardiomegaly (late)
  • Calcification of aortic valve
  • Pulmonary congestion (late)
  • Boot-shaped heart (late)

💡 Low-Flow Low-Gradient AS — The Tricky Scenario Some patients have severe AS but low gradient because the LV is failing and can't generate enough pressure. How to detect true severity?

→ Dobutamine stress echocardiography: If gradient increases with dobutamine = true severe AS (the valve is the problem). If gradient stays low = pseudo-severe AS (the LV is the problem).

Classic boards question: "Patient with AVA <1.0 cm² but mean gradient of 20 mmHg, EF of 25% — what test?"
🔬 Cardiac Catheterization

Reserved for when echo is inconclusive OR pre-operatively to check coronary anatomy (done before valve surgery in adults >40 years). The Gorlin formula calculates valve area from hemodynamic data.

⚠️ NOT first-line. Echo is first!

06. Management — Decisions, Decisions

🚨 THE MOST IMPORTANT MANAGEMENT FACT AS is a mechanical problem requiring a mechanical solution. No drug has been shown to alter the course of AS. Medical management is only for symptom control and bridge therapy.
Indications for AVR Mnemonic
SAVR

  • AAsymptomatic severe AS + EF <50% or undergoing other cardiac surgery

  • RRapid progression on serial echos + exercise testing abnormalities

💊 Medical Therapy — What NOT to Do (Equally Important!)
❌ CONTRAINDICATED • Vasodilators (nitrates, ACEi, ARBs) — can precipitate severe hypotension
• Vigorous diuretics — can drop preload dangerously
• Beta-blockers (in decompensated) — worsen CO
• Inotropes (unless bridge to surgery)
✅ ACCEPTABLE / USED • Diuretics (gentle) for congestion
• Beta-blockers for AF rate control
• Statins — observational benefit in calcific AS (unproven)
• Anticoagulation for AF
• Endocarditis prophylaxis (if required)
🎯 Balloon Valvuloplasty (BAV) — When is it used?
BAV is NOT a definitive treatment for adult AS — the stenosis recurs within 6–12 months.

Uses in adults: Bridge to SAVR/TAVR in hemodynamically unstable patients, palliative in non-surgical candidates, or during pregnancy (preferred over SAVR).

In children with congenital AS: BAV IS the preferred initial treatment!
🤰 AS in Pregnancy — High Yield Clinical Scenario Pregnancy is HIGH RISK in severe AS because:
• ↑cardiac output demand (~50% increase)
• ↓SVR of pregnancy → fixed output can't compensate → decompensation

Management: Close monitoring, avoid vigorous exercise, balloon valvuloplasty if intervention needed, avoid vasodilators/diuretics aggressively.

Q1
A 72-year-old man presents with exertional chest pain for 4 months and a single episode of fainting while climbing stairs. He has no history of CAD. Exam reveals a harsh systolic murmur at the 2nd RICS radiating to the carotids, slow-rising pulse, and a sustained apex beat.
What is the expected mean survival in this patient given his current dominant symptom?
A 1–2 years due to development of heart failure
B 5 years due to angina
C 3 years due to syncope
D 8 years, as symptoms are only mild
✅ Answer: C — 3 years (Syncope)
The dominant symptom here is syncope (fainting while climbing stairs = exertional syncope). Using the SAD mnemonic: Syncope → 3-year mean survival; Angina → 5-year; Dyspnea (HF) → 1–2 years.

Note: This patient also has angina, but the question asks about the dominant symptom listed — the syncope. The mechanism of exertional syncope in AS is: during exercise, fixed cardiac output + peripheral vasodilation (exercise) → sudden ↓BP → cerebral hypoperfusion. Also, baroreceptor reflex triggering vasovagal syncope may play a role.
Q2
A 65-year-old woman with known severe AS (AVA 0.8 cm², mean gradient 45 mmHg) is referred for evaluation. She has NO symptoms — no chest pain, no syncope, no breathlessness. Her ECG shows LVH. Echocardiography reveals EF of 42%.
What is the most appropriate next step?
A Watchful waiting with serial echos every 6 months
B Aortic valve replacement
C Start ACE inhibitor and diuretic
D Dobutamine stress echocardiography
✅ Answer: B — Aortic Valve Replacement
Even though she has NO symptoms, AVR is indicated because of EF <50% in the setting of severe AS. This is a Class I indication per guidelines.

Remember the SAVR mnemonic: "A = Asymptomatic severe AS + EF <50%" — this patient qualifies!

Option C is dangerous — ACEi can cause severe hypotension in AS (vasodilators are generally contraindicated). Dobutamine echo (D) is used in low-gradient AS to distinguish true severe from pseudo-severe — not indicated here where the gradient is clearly elevated (45 mmHg >40).
Q3
A 45-year-old man presents with exertional breathlessness. Auscultation reveals a systolic murmur at the left sternal border that INCREASES with Valsalva maneuver and standing, and DECREASES with squatting. He has a family history of sudden cardiac death.
Which of the following findings most differentiates this condition from aortic stenosis?
A Presence of LVH on ECG
B Murmur increases with Valsalva and standing
C History of exertional symptoms
D Sustained apex beat on examination
✅ Answer: B — Murmur increases with Valsalva and standing
This is HOCM (Hypertrophic Obstructive Cardiomyopathy). The key differentiator is dynamic obstruction in HOCM — anything that reduces LV filling (↓preload) increases obstruction and murmur loudness.

Valsalva → ↓venous return → ↓LV volume → LVOT obstruction worsens → murmur louder (HOCM)
Squatting → ↑venous return → ↑LV volume → obstruction lessens → murmur softer (HOCM)

In AS: the opposite — squatting/↑preload = louder murmur. LVH, exertional symptoms, and heaving apex can occur in BOTH conditions.
Q4
An 80-year-old woman with severe symptomatic AS is deemed high surgical risk due to severe COPD, prior stroke, and frailty. Her STS score is 12%. Echo confirms AVA 0.7 cm², mean gradient 50 mmHg, EF 55%.
What is the most appropriate intervention?
A Surgical AVR (SAVR)
B Transcatheter AVR (TAVR)
C Balloon aortic valvuloplasty as definitive therapy
D Conservative management with diuretics and ACE inhibitor
✅ Answer: B — TAVR
She is symptomatic severe AS + high surgical risk (STS >8% = high risk). This is the classic TAVR indication.

TAVR (Transcatheter AVR) = valve delivered percutaneously via femoral artery, avoiding open surgery. Landmark trials (PARTNER trials) showed TAVR superior to medical therapy in inoperable patients, and non-inferior to SAVR in high-risk patients.

BAV (C) is NOT definitive in adults — stenosis recurs within 6–12 months. Option D with ACEi is dangerous in AS (vasodilator → hypotension). Medical therapy has NOT been shown to improve survival in AS.
Q5
A 58-year-old man with severe AS (AVA 0.9 cm²) has EF of 28% and a mean gradient of only 18 mmHg. Echo shows a heavily calcified valve. You want to determine if the true valve area is indeed severe or if this is pseudo-severe AS from a failing LV.
Which investigation will best help you differentiate?
A CT calcium scoring of the aortic valve
B Exercise stress echocardiography
C Low-dose dobutamine stress echocardiography
D Cardiac MRI with gadolinium
✅ Answer: C — Low-dose dobutamine stress echo
This is the classic Low-Flow Low-Gradient (LFLG) AS scenario.

When EF is severely reduced, the LV can't generate enough force to create a high gradient — so the gradient appears low DESPITE severe AS. This can be "pseudo-severe" where the valve is actually OK but looks small because the LV isn't opening it fully.

Dobutamine stress echo interpretation:
• ↑ gradient + ↑ SV → True severe AS (fixed anatomic stenosis, valve is the culprit → AVR)
• No ↑ gradient, small ↑ SV → Pseudo-severe AS (valve opens more, cardiomyopathy is the real problem)

Note: Exercise stress echo is contraindicated in severe symptomatic AS.
Q6
During cardiac exam of a 70-year-old woman with known AS, you notice that the murmur is loud and harsh at the base but at the cardiac apex, it sounds musical, almost like a cooing dove. The apex beat is sustained and non-displaced.
What is this phenomenon called, and what is the mechanism?
A Austin Flint murmur — AR jet causing functional MS
B Carey-Coombs murmur — rheumatic inflammation of MV
C Gallavardin phenomenon — musical component of AS murmur transmitted to apex
D Concurrent mitral regurgitation due to annular calcification
✅ Answer: C — Gallavardin Phenomenon
The Gallavardin phenomenon is a classic exam favourite! In elderly patients with calcific AS, the murmur has two components:

1. Harsh component heard at the base (aortic area) — transmitted to carotids
2. Musical/high-frequency component at the apex — sounds like MR or "cooing dove"

The KEY: This is NOT concurrent MR. The musical component is just the high-frequency component of the same AS murmur being filtered as it radiates to the apex. The sustained, undisplaced apex beat confirms hypertrophy (no dilation), arguing against significant MR.
Q7
A 35-year-old pregnant woman (24 weeks) with known moderate AS develops progressive exertional dyspnea. Her resting echo shows mean gradient 32 mmHg, AVA 1.2 cm², EF 60%. She is on no medications.
What is the most appropriate management?
A Emergency SAVR with cardiopulmonary bypass
B Start enalapril and furosemide
C Cautious diuretics for symptoms; balloon valvuloplasty if deterioration continues
D Immediate TAVR
✅ Answer: C — Cautious diuretics; balloon valvuloplasty if needed
Pregnancy + AS is a high-stakes scenario. The patient has moderate AS (not severe), so immediate surgery is not warranted. EF is preserved.

Management ladder in pregnancy with AS:
1. Activity restriction + monitoring
2. Gentle diuretics for symptomatic relief (avoid aggressive diuresis — reduces preload)
3. Beta-blockers for rate control if needed
4. If severe decompensation: balloon valvuloplasty is preferred (avoids open heart surgery risks to fetus)
5. SAVR only if life-threatening deterioration (high fetal mortality with bypass)

ACEi/ARBs (B) are TERATOGENIC — absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy!
Q8
A medical student auscultates a patient and hears: a normal S1, a murmur that starts slightly after S1, crescendos to a peak in mid-systole, then decrescendos and ends before S2. S2 appears soft. There is a systolic thrill at the 2nd RICS. The murmur radiates to both carotids.
A second-year student asks: "How is this different from MR murmur?" What is the BEST distinguishing feature?
A MR murmur is louder and harsher
B MR murmur is pansystolic (S1 to S2), whereas AS murmur is ejection systolic (starts after S1, ends before S2)
C MR murmur radiates to the carotids
D AS murmur is best heard at the apex
✅ Answer: B — Pansystolic (MR) vs Ejection Systolic (AS)
This is a fundamentally important auscultation concept:

AS (Ejection Systolic Murmur):
• Starts AFTER S1 (there's an opening click or gap)
• Crescendo-decrescendo (diamond shaped)
• Ends BEFORE S2 (gap before S2)
• Reason: Blood is EJECTED during systole through a narrow valve

MR (Pansystolic / Holosystolic Murmur):
• Starts WITH S1 (replaces S1)
• Uniform intensity throughout
• Goes up to and may obscure S2
• Reason: Any pressure gradient from LV to LA exists throughout ALL of systole

MR radiates to the axilla, not carotids. AS murmur is best heard at the 2nd RICS, not the apex.

Rapid Fire Pearls 🔥

🚫 NEVER Give in AS • Vasodilators (GTN, ACEi, ARBs, hydralazine)
• Vigorous diuretics
• Inotropes (except as bridge)
• Exercise stress test in symptomatic severe AS
✅ Always Remember • Echo = investigation of choice
• AVR = only definitive treatment
• Dyspnea = worst prognosis (1–2 yr)
• TAVR = high-risk elderly patients
💡 Associations • Heyde syndrome = AS + GI angiodysplasia + bleeding (acquired vWD type 2A)
• Bicuspid AV + Coarctation + Turner
• AS + AR = Rheumatic fever
⚡ Calculation Pearls • Gorlin formula = valve area (cath lab)
• Continuity equation = AVA on echo
• Bernoulli: ΔP = 4V² (peak gradient)
Final Revision Summary
Cause → Calcific (old) / Bicuspid (young) / Rheumatic (always with MR)
Pathophys → Pressure overload → Concentric LVH → ↓compliance → LVEDP↑
Symptoms → SAD (Syncope 3yr, Angina 5yr, Dyspnea 1–2yr)
Signs → Slow-rising pulse, PASS, narrow pulse pressure, ESM → carotids
Echo → Severe = AVA <1.0, gradient >40, velocity >4 (the 1-4-4 rule)
Mx → Surgery (SAVR young/low risk, TAVR elderly/high risk); NO vasodilators
